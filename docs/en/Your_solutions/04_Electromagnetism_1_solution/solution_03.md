
## 3. Electrostatic Equilibrium
**The Problem:** We have two positive charges on a line: +4C on the left, and +9C on the right. They are 2 meters apart. We want to place a third positive charge (+1C) somewhere between them so it stays perfectly still (equilibrium). Where should we put it?

**Step-by-step Explanation:**
1.  **The Logic:** The +4C pushes the new charge to the right. The +9C pushes it to the left. To make it stay still, the left push must exactly equal the right push. Because +9C is much stronger, the new charge needs to be placed *further away* from the +9C and *closer* to the +4C to make the pushes equal.
2.  **Define the Distances:**
    Let's say the distance from the +4C charge is $x$.
    Since the total distance is 2 meters, the distance from the +9C charge will be $(2 - x)$.
3.  **Set the Forces Equal:** Coulomb's Law formula for force is $F = k \frac{q_1 \cdot q_2}{r^2}$.
    $$ \text{Push from Left} = \text{Push from Right} $$
    $$ k \frac{4 \cdot 1}{x^2} = k \frac{9 \cdot 1}{(2-x)^2} $$
4.  **Solve the Math:**
    Cancel out $k$ and the $1$s on both sides to clean it up:
    $$ \frac{4}{x^2} = \frac{9}{(2-x)^2} $$
    Here is a great math trick. Take the square root of both sides to remove the "squared" ($^2$) parts. (The square root of 4 is 2, the square root of 9 is 3):
    $$ \frac{2}{x} = \frac{3}{2-x} $$
    Now, cross-multiply (multiply top-left with bottom-right, and top-right with bottom-left):
    $$ 2(2 - x) = 3x $$
    $$ 4 - 2x = 3x $$
    Move the $-2x$ to the right side by adding it:
    $$ 4 = 5x $$
    Divide by 5:
    $$ x = 0.8 \text{ meters} $$

**Final Answer:** You must place the charge 0.8 meters away from the +4C charge.
